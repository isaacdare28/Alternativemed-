Module 1: Foundations of Herbal Medicine Not Started

Historical Context

Herbal medicine dates back over 60,000 years, with evidence found in Neanderthal burial sites. Major traditions include Traditional Chinese Medicine (5000 BCE), Ayurveda (3000 BCE), and Western herbalism documented by Dioscorides (1st century CE).

Philosophical Approaches

Key concepts across traditions:

  • Traditional Chinese Medicine: Yin-Yang balance, Qi flow, and the Five Elements
  • Ayurveda: Doshas (Vata, Pitta, Kapha) and the concept of Prakriti
  • Western Herbalism: Doctrine of Signatures and humoral theory

Modern Scientific Perspective

Contemporary research validates many traditional uses through phytochemistry, identifying active compounds like salicin (willow bark → aspirin) and artemisinin (sweet wormwood → malaria treatment).

Module 2: Major Herbal Traditions Locked

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

Key Concepts

Focus on balancing Qi through herbs classified by temperature (hot, warm, neutral, cool, cold) and taste (pungent, sweet, sour, bitter, salty).

Signature Herbs

  • Ginseng (Ren Shen): Adaptogen for energy
  • Astragalus (Huang Qi): Immune support
  • Dang Gui: Women's health

Ayurveda

Key Concepts

Herbs classified by their effects on doshas (Vata, Pitta, Kapha) and their rasa (taste), virya (energy), and vipaka (post-digestive effect).

Signature Herbs

  • Ashwagandha: Stress adaptogen
  • Turmeric: Anti-inflammatory
  • Triphala: Digestive tonic

Western Herbalism

Key Concepts

Focus on organ systems and physiological effects, with herbs classified as nervines, alteratives, hepatics, etc.

Signature Herbs

  • Echinacea: Immune support
  • St. John's Wort: Nervous system
  • Milk Thistle: Liver support

Herb-Drug Interactions

Important Interactions

  • St. John's Wort: Reduces effectiveness of birth control, antidepressants
  • Ginkgo: Increases bleeding risk with blood thinners
  • Licorice: Raises blood pressure with diuretics

Dose-Response Relationships

Understanding therapeutic windows - from infusion (1-2 tsp/cup) to tincture (1:5 ratio) to standardized extracts (e.g., 240mg Ginkgo with 24% flavonoids).

Module 4: Preparation Methods Locked

Basic Preparation Techniques

Infusions

For delicate parts (flowers, leaves): Pour boiling water, steep 5-15 minutes (e.g., chamomile tea).

Decoctions

For hard parts (roots, bark): Simmer 15-45 minutes (e.g., ginger root tea).

Tinctures

Alcohol extracts (typically 1:5 ratio in 40-60% alcohol), macerate 2-6 weeks (e.g., echinacea tincture).

Advanced Preparations

Standardized Extracts

Concentrated to specific active compounds (e.g., 95% curcumin from turmeric).

Essential Oils

Steam distillation of volatile compounds (e.g., lavender oil).

Poultices/Compresses

Fresh or dried herbs applied topically (e.g., plantain for insect bites).

Module 5: Clinical Applications Locked

Common Conditions and Herbal Protocols

Stress/Anxiety

  • Ashwagandha: Adaptogen (300-500mg standardized extract)
  • Passionflower: Nervine (2-4ml tincture 3x daily)
  • Lemon Balm: Calming (1-3 cups tea daily)

Digestive Issues

  • Peppermint: IBS (0.2-0.4ml enteric-coated oil)
  • Ginger: Nausea (1-2g dried root)
  • Chamomile: Indigestion (3-4 cups tea daily)

Immune Support

  • Echinacea: Prevention (2-3ml tincture 3x daily)
  • Elderberry: Viral infections (1Tbsp syrup 4x daily)
  • Astragalus: Long-term support (500mg capsule 2x daily)

Case Study: Insomnia Protocol

45-year-old female with sleep maintenance insomnia:

  1. Valerian root: 300-600mg at bedtime (GABA modulation)
  2. Chamomile tea: 1 cup before bed (apigenin effects)
  3. Lavender oil: Aromatherapy (linalool content)

Expected results: Improved sleep latency within 1 week, better sleep maintenance by week 3.

Module 6: Safety, Ethics, and Practice Locked

Safety Considerations

Contraindicated Herbs

  • Ephedra: Cardiovascular risks
  • Comfrey: Liver toxicity (pyrrolizidine alkaloids)
  • Kava: Liver damage risk

Special Populations

  • Pregnancy: Avoid black cohosh, pennyroyal
  • Children: Adjust doses (Clark's rule: child dose = (age/(age+12)) × adult dose
  • Elderly: Monitor for herb-drug interactions

Ethical Practice

  • Scope of Practice: Know legal limitations in your region
  • Informed Consent: Disclose potential risks/benefits
  • Sustainability: Use ethically sourced herbs (United Plant Savers at-risk list)

Starting an Herbal Practice

  1. Obtain proper training/certification
  2. Create standard intake forms
  3. Develop apothecary with 20-30 core herbs
  4. Establish referral network with MDs/NDs
  5. Maintain continuing education
